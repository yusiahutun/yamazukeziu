4-year-old girl who was shot in Chicago leaves hospital

A four-year-old Chicago girl who was shot in the head 10 days ago was discharged from the hospital Monday as her mother pleaded for an end to violence in the city.

'Stop killing our children in our city please,' said Trennetta Greshman, 29, who was planning to move the family to Minneapolis. 

'Please put the guns down because you're killing our future and our city.'

Jacele Johnson, wearing a bright pink coat, waved to onlookers and journalists as she was released from Chicago's Comer Children's Hospital. 

Recovering: Four-year-old Jacele Johnson, who was shot in the head on May 22 outside a prom party in Chicago's Englewood neighborhood, is released from Comer Childrens Hospital on Monday, June 1, 2015

Johnson was put in an ambulance that would take her to a Minnesota hospital where she will undergo further treatment. Police say she was not the target of the shooting

Mom Trenetta Gresham spoke with reporters, calling for an end to Chicago's violence, as her daughter was released from hospital after 10 days 

She had visible staples in her head and was put in an ambulance that would take her to a Minnesota hospital where she will undergo further treatment.

The girl was shot May 22 outside a prom party in Chicago's Englewood neighborhood.

Jacele was not the target of the shooting, authorities said.

A 17-year-old male, believed to be the little girl's cousin, was also shot in the neck and chest.

He was listed as in a serious condition upon being admitted.

A 15-year-old girl was also hit.

Jacele Johnson was sitting in a car outside a party in Engelwood when she was hit in the head by a bullet 

Scene: The shooting occurred about 8pm in the 7000 block of South Justine Street in Englewood

Gresham called her daughter a fighter, saying 'I never gave up on her and she never gave up on me.'

Jacele had been admitted in critical condition after the shooting and was upgraded to fair condition. 

 'It was a close call - not so much the bullet, which didn't penetrate that deeply into the brain - but the bone was actually penetrating and pushing on the brain and ruptured vessels,' Comer neurosurgeon Dr. Peter Warnk told ABC 7.
