Chart reveals how much clothing sizes have changed over the past 60 years - and shows size 12 Marily

It’s been argued again and again, but one man has finally turned up proof that Marilyn Monroe was not a size 12 – at least not by today’s standards, that is.

The oft-repeated factoid that the curvy bombshell wore clothing in a US size 12 – or UK size 16 – has been used to signify the way beauty standards have changed over the past 50 or so years. But while Marilyn’s clothing tags did in fact include double-digit numbers, a size 12 in 1958 was a lot smaller than it is today.

Max Galka, a writer from New York, has created two handy graphs showing just what clothing sizes women have worn based on their waist and bust sizes since 1958 – and it seems that by today’s sizing, the Gentleman Prefer Blondes star could actually be as small as a double-zero.

New standards: A chart using data from the National Institute of Standards and Technology and the American Society of Testing and Materials shows how clothing sizes have changed over the past several decades

While bigger women now fit in smaller-numbered sizes, there's also a wider range of sizes available

The graphs were created using data from the National Institute of Standards and Technology and the American Society of Testing and Materials.

It has been reported that Marliyn’s waist was between 22” and 25”, and her bust size was between 34” and 37”.

So, truly, in 1958 – a year before Some Like it Hot hit the big screen - a dress made to fit her waist would have been between a size eight and a size 12. A dress that perfectly fit her bust would have been between a size 12 and a size 16. 

But if the 5’5.5”, 118lb. star were alive today, according to the data represented in Max’s graphs, her bust would fit best in a size four to a size 10 – and her waist would fit best in a size double-zero.

The wide range of possible sizes that the blonde stunner would be able to fit into in 2015 can be partially attributed to the fact that there is a greater range of sizes available

There also aren't definitive measurements for the star, whose weight may have also fluctuated over the course of her career.

But it’s indisputable that Marilyn would wear a much smaller dress if she were around today. 

Max attributes this to the fact that Americans have become increasingly overweight and obese over the years. In 1960, the average woman weighed just 140lbs. In 2010, she weighed 166.2lbs – about the same as the average man weighed back in 1960. 

Curvy girl: At 5’5.5” and 118lb, Marilyn was actually quite thin - but had a larger-than-average bust

'Everyone knows people have gotten bigger, but until you see the actual numbers laid out in front of you, it is hard to appreciate how big the change actually is,' Max, who admits that he himself was surprised by the numbers, told Daily Mail Online.

As people have gotten bigger, a larger range of sizes has needed to be created to accommodate them. Vanity sizing has also played a part here, with garments being labelled with smaller numbers to make women with growing waists feel smaller and thinner.

'Clothing manufacturers do it intentionally because it helps them sell clothes,' Max went on. 'There has been a lot of research on vanity sizing, and when people fit into a smaller size, it raises their self esteem and makes them more likely to buy. 

'In that regard, clothing cuts probably would have gotten bigger to some degree even if people had not. But I think most of the change in sizing is a result of clothing manufacturers trying to keep up with a growing customer base.'
