Understanding Your Heart Rate Zones Is Important

If you own an Apple Watch or fitness tracker from Fitbit, Garmin, WHOOP — the list goes on — you already have access to your heart rate with the simple flick of the wrist or bop of a button. However, unless you know how to actually use that data, you're missing out on myriad benefits of heart rate training (and not getting the most out of your fancy wearable either).

An exercise imperative that involves eyeing your ticker while you train, heart rate training has the power to help you reach your fitness goals as efficiently and quickly as possible. That stands whether you want to lose weight, PR a race or lift, or reach your athletic potential right from your yoga mat or bike. Intrigued? We reached out to experts Dr. Jason M. Hoff, Mike Leveque, Dr. Brittany Masteller, and Dr. Dave Hoppe for more information on using your heart rate zones to improve your workout and overall health.

What Is Heart Rate Training?

At its most basic, heart rate training involves keeping your heart rate — measured by the number of times your heart beats per minute (BPM) — within a certain range for a designated period of time.

The Benefits of Heart Rate Training

The main benefit of heart rate training is its cardiovascular training, Dr. Hoff told us. Defined as any vigorous activity that increases heart rate, cardiovascular training offers health perks ranging from improved heart health and lower blood pressure to reduced risk of type 2 diabetes.

The American Heart Association recommends getting at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise or 75 minutes per week of vigorous aerobic exercise. The problem is that only half of people get this much exercise, which should be considered the bare minimum, noted Dr. Hoff.

According to a study, researchers from Brigham and Women's Hospital, in collaboration with the American Heart Association and Apple, found that after analyzing more than 18 million workouts logged using Apple Watch during the pandemic, participants with above-average Cardio Fitness levels (a strong indicator of your overall physical health) averaged more than 200 minutes of activity per week, whereas those with high Cardio Fitness averaged more than 300 minutes of activity per week — the number Dr. Hoff suggested aiming for before adding that paying attention to your heart rate can motivate people who are under-exercising to exercise more.

Heart rate training also requires paying attention to your heart rate more or less continuously throughout your workout. 

"Heart rate training allows you to monitor the intensity of your workout in real-time," explained Leveque. 

Translation: You're able to go harder or slower based on what heart rate you need to be in to reach your goals. If your goal is just to finish a marathon, for example, you'll probably spend most of your training time in zone 2. However, if you want to PR your marathon, you'll need to train with a little more chutzpah — meaning, log some miles in zones 3 and 4.

Even if you don't look at and monitor mid-movement, Leveque said wearing a heart rate monitor encourages more self-reflection post-workout. According to Leveque, in addition to your heart rate, most heart rate monitors tell you how many calories you've burned, minute-by-minute intensity graphing, average intensity, and gamification metrics or points earned. 

"This greater feedback can encourage you to keep making progress toward your goal," Leveque added.

Heart Rate Zones

As it goes, there are five main ranges that someone's heart rate could fall within. These ranges are referred to as Zone 1, Zone 2, Zone 3, Zone 4, and Zone 5. As you might guess, the higher the zone number, the higher the heart rate range.

These ranges aren't specific numbers but rather percent ranges of your max heart rate based on your age, according to Dr. Masteller. The reason the zones are based on age-predicted maximum hearts is that peoples' (healthy) maximum heart rate changes as they age — older hearts simply can't beat as fast as younger hearts.

According to the American College of Sports Medicine, the five zones are broken down as follows:

  • Zone 1 (<57 percent age - predicted max heart rate)
  • Zone 2 (57-63 percent age - predicted max heart rate)
  • Zone 3 (64-76 percent age - predicted max heart rate)
  • Zone 4 (77-95 percent age - predicted max heart rate)
  • Zone 5 (95 percent - maximum age-predicted max heart rate).

Zone 1

"Zone 1 is the zone you're in during very light activity," said Dr. Hoff. 

Think: nature walking, lower-intensity hikes, golf, foam rolling, restorative yoga, etc. Generally, this is the zone individuals stay within during recovery days.

Training within this zone can help the body become more efficient at delivering oxygen to your muscles and carrying lactic acid (that's science-speak for "exercise waste") away from the muscles. Over time, this allows people to exercise longer and harder without having to deal with that burning feeling lactic acid build-up creates.

Zone 2

If you've heard of heart rate training, it's likely because you've heard of Zone 2 training, which is the most popular iteration of heart rate training. Specifically popular amongst CrossFit athletes, Zone 2 training is the lowest zone used for training purposes.

"It's a pace you can go at and sustain for a long period of time," explained Dr. Hoppe. For most people, that means going for a 30 to 60-minute jog, but this can also be done at a stationary rower, ski urg, assault bike, or other stationary bike.

In addition to reducing the risk of injury and improving insulin resistance,  Zone 2 training has been shown to increase VO2 max, which is how much oxygen your body can utilize. The higher your VO2 max, the more physically fit a person is, per research. In practice, that means you get less tired doing the same types of exercise. 

"Training in this range helps to speed up recovery after heavier exercises as well," added Dr. Hoff.

Zone 3

Zone 3 is where a person may begin to feel the burn, suggested Dr. Hoppe. If you've ever taken a SoulCycle class, your legs know exactly the muscular burn he's talking about.

Over time, staying in this zone can become a challenge, but it's a pace that is still manageable for distance or duration work, he says. This is the heart rate most people usually stay in during the majority of Pilates classes, kickboxing lessons, and run club runs.

"Zone 3 is the zone that helps improve aerobic fitness to help a person build up their aerobic strength," said Dr. Hoppe. 

It can also improve anaerobic threshold, meaning that the more you train in Zone 3, the higher the intensity you'll be able to entail for a longer period of time.

Zone 4

In this zone your lungs and heart will burn baby burn. According to Dr. Hoff, that's because, to get into the nitty-gritty, in this zone you're creating waste faster than your body can feel it. 

"You'll feel the burn in your muscles, and, as such, it will be difficult to stay in this zone long," explained Dr. Hoff.

The most iconic example of Zone 4 training is a 5K run race or 500-meter swim. Other common places include CrossFit and HIIT classes and other high-intensity training.

Don't let the hurt of this hot spot scare you, though. 

"This is the zone where you push the limits of your exercise threshold and gradually increase your aerobic capacity performance," Dr. Hoff added.

Zone 5

"Zone 5 is the zone you're in where you are pushing yourself to your limit," noted Dr. Hoff. 

It's the zone you're in when you feel like you're about to red-line. As in, no longer than one to two minutes

"You won't be able to hang around in this zone very long," Dr. Hoff continued.

Races like the 200-meter swim, 400-meter dash, or the CrossFit Fran workout will probably put you there.

Training in this zone can help you increase power and speed, strengthen muscles, and help you become more proficient at training at high intensities.

How to Find Your Maximum Heart Rate

To calculate an estimate of your maximum heart rate, simply subtract your age from 220. That means that if you're 25, your maximum heart rate is estimated to be 195, while if you're 35, it's 185. Another popular option for finding your max heart rate is something called the Tanaka equation, advised Dr. Masteller, which uses the formula: 208 - (0.7 x age).

Once you know your estimated max heart rate, you can do a little math to figure out what your heart rate floor and ceiling should be to stay within different zones.

How to Try Heart Rate Training

1. Figure Out Your Fitness Goals

Heart rate training isn't about getting your heart rate as high as possible — and training too much in your Zone 4 or 5 can actually make it hard for your body to properly recover. It's really all about training smarter based on your own goals. So ask yourself: What do I want to accomplish at the gym?

For example, if you're training for short and fast exercises like a 100-meter race, you're going to be doing more high-intensity or interval training in Zone 4 or 5, noted Dr. Hoff. Meanwhile, people training to run a marathon are pushing the limits of their endurance and will want to stay in lower zones but for much longer periods.

2. Get a Heart Rate Monitor

The easiest way to heart zone train is with the help of a heart rate monitor. Chest straps and armband monitors tend to be slightly more accurate, suggested Dr. Hoff, but wristbands like Whoop, Polar, Garmin, and Apple Watch all have more intel and fun features.

"Pick the monitor that works best with your body and workouts," Dr. Hoff said. "After all, it does not matter how accurate the monitor is if it bothers you during workouts and you end up leaving it at home."

3. Learn How to Gauge Your Heart Rate

Want to make sure you actually enjoy heart rate training before dropping dough on a monitor? Dr. Masteller recommended trying a simple talk test to gauge intensity. 

"Someone performing moderate intensity (Zone 2 and 3) activity should be able to carry on a conversation without being out of breath," Dr. Masteller noted. However, they shouldn't be able to sing (that's Zone 1).

"If someone is doing vigorous-intensity activity (Zone 4), they will not be able to say more than a few words without needing to pause and take a breath," Dr. Masteller said. "This intensity will feel very challenging but doable."

As for Zone 5? The reality is if you can speak more than a word or two, you're not pushing hard enough to be in Zone 5.

4. Listen to Your Body

Indeed, there's a time and a place for heart rate training. 

"But it's still important to remember to listen to your body," said Dr. Hoff. That means not narrowing your attention to your heart rate monitor and making sure to also pay attention to your body and any potential signs of excessive fatigue or injury. (No matter what, he says, "Stop if you feel like something is wrong.")

For instance, just because your heart rate isn't sky-high doesn't mean your body isn't exhausted. Lack of energy, uncharacteristic moodiness and anger, poor sleep quality, an increase in nightmares, and decreased motivation are all signs of overtraining (aka you should take a day or three off).
