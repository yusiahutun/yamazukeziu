Champion Australian skydiver Melissa Porter's tragic mistake before tragic death in Houston, Texas

A champion Australian skydiver plummeted to her death when she attempted a high risk manoeuvre too close to landing, sending her crashing to the ground before shocked onlookers.

Melissa Porter, 29, from Port Kennedy, south of Perth, died on June 24 at the Skydive Spaceland facility south of Houston, Texas, where she worked as an instructor.

It's now been revealed that after an uneventful free fall from the plane, Ms Porter attempted an advanced move known as 'swooping' too close to the ground.

Swooping is an advanced form of canopy piloting, where a diver induces speed by diving the canopy toward the earth while leaving time to level out and glide horizontally across the ground.

Spectators watched on as Ms Porter suffered a hard landing on the ground before tumbling into a nearby shallow body of water.

She was rushed to hospital where she was pronounced dead.

It's understood Ms Porter's body suffered no blunt force trauma.

Investigators are yet to determine whether she had a medical emergency during the dive or died from impact.

A memorial event is now be planned at the Skydive Spaceland Houston facility,  where Ms Porter had vast experience with the conditions and equipment.

Champion skydiver Mel Porter died after she suffered a hard landing on the ground before falling into a nearby shallow body of water

The facility has ruled out weather and equipment as factors in the tragedy.

'Melissa Porter, 29, of Rosharon, had done several hundred successful skydives at this location over the last two years and was using her own parachute system for a self-supervised skydive,' Skydive Spaceland Houston told 7NEWS.com.au.

'The free fall was uneventful and the parachute deployed and functioned normally, but witnesses reported that she initiated an advanced landing manoeuvre too close to the ground for a safe landing.'

'We are devastated by the loss of our good friend, and we offer our sincerest condolences to her family and friends, 

The Galveston County Medical Examiner's Office has launched an investigation into what caused Ms Porter's death.

The tragedy has rocked the skydiving community around the world and sparked outpouring of tributes.

Ms Porter's mum Vonnie fought back tears as she paid tribute to her 'baby girl' on Monday.

'She was just the nicest person you could ever meet. She was so caring and warm and kind,' she told Seven News.

Australia's skydiving community has been rocked by shock death of Melissa Porter

The Aussie skydiver has previously performed hundreds of successful dives at the Skydive Spaceland Houston facility (pictured) in Texas

'She just treated people with so much compassion and I've had so many messages from people that she's helped through dark times.

'She was my rock and I was hers. You couldn't ask for a nicer little girl.'

Skydiving was a childhood dream for Ms Porter and became her career too.

'She died doing what she loved,' her mum told Nine News.

'She loved it. It was her happy place.'

Vonnie and her son will travel to the US this weekend to attend the memorial skydive and to bring Mel's body home.

The celebration of Ms Porter's life will be held at Skydive Spaceland Houston next Tuesday (July 11).

'Mel was a magical person who touched the hearts of many people in the 40 countries she visited in her 29 years,' skydiving colleague Nicholas Lott wrote.

'She had finally found a place that felt like home here in Texas, but unfortunately she wasn't able to stay as long as any of us would have hoped. 

'Those close to her have found great solace in the fact that she passed doing what she truly loved.'

Ms Porter had spent the last month in Texas preparing for the jump and recently shared footage of herself training at iFLY El Paso. 

'Finally sent some head down in the tunnel,' Ms Porter captioned the video.

Fellow skydivers have taken to social media to express their grief and pay tribute.

'We lost such a beautiful person. Rest peacefully Mel,' one wrote.

Others remembered Ms Porter as 'exuberant' and an inspiration to other young female divers.

'Skydiving was her life,' coach Kristina Hicks told The West Australian.

'She was loving (living in Texas) and being part of the diving community over there where there were opportunities to progress and work with some of the best divers in the world.'

Melissa Porter was passionate about inspiring girl to take up the adrenaline sport

The  worldly traveller who visited 40 countries always enjoyed a taste of home

The professional skydiver had recently returned  to the US to work as an instructor

Weeks before she flew to Texas, Ms Porter was among 23 women who jumped at Skydive Ramblers in Toogoolawah to complete a '2-point 23 way', an Australian women's total-break sequential record.

She and her skydiving partner won gold in the two-way Inter Vertical Formation Skydiving category at the national championships in WA's Wheatbelt region in March.

She fell in love with the adrenaline sport after watching her grandmother skydive and took her first tandem skydive on her 14th birthday.

Ms Porter has spent the last five years travelling the world and participated in several skydiving adventures,  She also learned to solo skydive in Portugal.

In March, Ms Porter spoke about her passion to inspire more young women to pursue skydiving.   

'For so long the sport has been male-dominated,' she told the Sound Telegraph.

'There's this idea that women are too afraid or they're too small, or they're too light, all of these things and it's absolutely not true, they are 100 per cent capable.

'It's really cool for me to be able to be an instructor and to show other women it's doable.

'I want to continue doing that for years.'

A memorial skydive will be held in Houston, Texas next week in honour of Melissa Porter 

The Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade is providing consular assistance to Ms Porter's devastated family.

'We send our deepest condolences to the woman's family,' a DFAT spokesperson added.

An online fundraiser to support Ms Porter's loved ones and cover the significant costs of transporting her remains back home has already raised almost $15,000.

'Please help support Mel Porter's family in this challenging time. We're hoping that the financial burden of last minute international travel and funeral expenses can be the least of their worries as they begin to recover from this devastating loss,' the page states.

The fundraiser has been inundated with tributes.

'Mel was a bright shining light, an inspiration, and the best kind of friend I could have asked for. I'll miss her,' one man wrote.

Another friend added: 'We love you Mel. Thank you for being there on my very first jump. Will remember your coaching and the lessons you taught for ever.'
