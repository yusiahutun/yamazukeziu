Arrow to the heart: Darts walk-on-girls on being axed from jobs following controversy

Daniella Allfree and Charlotte Wood were axed from their role accompanying the darts players to the oche in January.

It sparked a huge political-correctness controversy as the Formula 1 grid girls were also dropped.

But now cheerleaders and dancers in the US are coming under the same scrutiny - and face the same fate.

Charlotte has been interviewed for HBO show Real Sports, which examined whether these roles should continue or whether they are merely decorative.

"It's happening over in the States like it did with us," says Charlotte. "It's political correctness gone mad."

Professional Darts Corporation boss Barry Hearn was also interviewed for the programme where he admitted the same, but said: "That's the world we live in now".

But for Daniella and Charlotte, it meant they lost a lot of money as working as walk-on girls brought in about 60% of their earnings.

Daniella says: "We have lost income. We are self-employed and the work comes as and when.

"It's not the steady wage we had working with the PDC."

The pair also noticed how the players missed them at the recent World Matchplay tournament in Blackpool one of the biggest on the darts circuit.

Daniella says: "Watching it on TV, it didn't look like the same show.

"Even the darts players said they felt lonely with-without the walk-on girls. A couple of players actually tripped up as they walked on because we weren't there.

"A lot of them are very nervous before a big match like that, so it was nice for them to have someone beside them to walk to the stage.

"Surely it must feel different for them after all these years of having someone with them for their entrance?

"Some players are really quite shy so I think it did help them."

Daniella and Charlotte are still popular at exhibition matches, where they have been overwhelmed by support from fans and players.

"Just speaking to the players on the circuit at exhibitions, they're saying how much they miss us," adds Daniella.

"We've had a lot of response from players and we still get support from fans on social media - women as well as men."

This prompted the pair to make their third calendar, which is out for pre-order now and which, sadly for fans, will be their last.

"We didn't think there would be a demand for it," says Daniella.

"But we put out a post on Instagram and got thousands of responses asking us to do it.

"One of the darts players, Vincent van der Voort, has bought a massive batch.

"He bought some last year and they sold really well so this year he told us he wanted loads for his shop in Holland."

Initially the pair were upset but were amazed at the support they had when they were dropped.

But eight months on, they still haven't had a proper explanation, and feel resentment at the decision. They had been a fixture of the darts circuit - Daniella for six years and Charlotte for three and a half - and had even been assured their jobs were safe.

"There's still not been a formal explanation or statement," says Daniella.

“We've felt more bitter about it as time has gone on.

"We were treated harshly - dropped from a job we put a lot of time into, without explanation.

"We were told we were doing a good job and would be with them for a long time but then we were dropped."

It's been made even worse for Daniella and Charlotte as walk-on girls still work at PDC tournaments on the Continent.

On the website, there's even a guide to the walk-on girls, with a section on how you can become one.

"It's still the PDC, it's still the same company and it's still being broadcast on ITV, on UK television," says Daniella. "Fans can still see the girls do walk-ons in European events.

"It is baffling to think why would they still have them in those countries but not in the UK?

"And there are ring girls at boxing matches and the dancers at the darts - who wear a lot less than we did.

"So I don't know why we were dropped and they stayed."

The models have also been selling off the dresses they wore as walk-on girls, which have been snapped up by fans.

"There's a big demand for them," explains Daniella.

"The last one I sold had 50 bids and the winner wanted it signed and with a note of authenticity. He's going to frame it and put it up in his darts room.

"He's got our calendars there and players' shirts.

"We were an iconic part of the darts and people want to hold on to that memory.

"Now we're gone, there may never be another walk-on girl in the UK."
