Supermodel Karen Elson feared saying no to nude shoots at just 16 would kill her career

Supermodel Karen Elson has revealed in her new memoir The Red Flame how she feared saying no to nude photo shoots at just 16 and discusses the behind-the-scenes reality of the fashion industry.  

The 41-year-old mother-of-two also admitted during an interview with The Sunday Times that for the first time in her life she has eaten 'what she actually wanted to' during lockdown. 

Karen, who has had an impressive career in fashion spanning 25 years, speaks openly in her latest book about the pressures of modelling and the effects it had on her body image.

Supermodel Karen Elson has had an impressive 25-year career in fashion but it has not been without its difficulties. She reveals all about her experiences in her latest book The Red Flame. Pictured: Karen walking for Galliano Fall/Winter in the 1999/2000 show (left) and in the winners room during The Fashion Awards 2019

As young as 16, Karen was posing for nude photo shoots and feared what effect saying no would have on her career. Pictured: Karen (left) with Iris Palmer at Paris Fashion Show in 1997

As young as 16 she was posing for nude photo shoots in Paris and said the British phrase 'Don't bite the hand that feeds you' spurred her on, despite the lack of compassion and regular criticism of her physique. 

Although the shoots were not sexual, she recalled people were more interested in the image than protecting the person in it and did not seem to realise she, a 16-year-old girl, was naked.

In February last year, Elson referenced a 'traumatizing experience' with singer Ryan Adams during the #MeToo scandal, after his ex-wife Mandy Moore claimed he was psychologically abusive toward her in their seven-year marriage.

Elson opened up on Instagram in a post where she used the hashtag #TimesUp to say other people speaking about their sexual misconduct scandals had encouraged her to speak out. However, at the time she added that she was not ready to share specific details on what happened between her and the now 45-year-old she provided musical support for on tour in 2017.

Her eye-opening debut book, which is available in October and is what she describes as far from the 'typical model book', opens up about the difficult times in her career.

One example she speaks of is from one of her first fashion shoots - several people called her 'fatty' after an editor told her she had eaten too much when a dress didn't fit her.

The supermodel told The Sunday Times that lockdown has been the first time in her life she has allowed herself to eat whatever she wanted without fear of how it would affect her career. Pictured: Karen at the New York Met Gala in 2019

She said the British phrase 'Don't bite the hand that feeds you' spurred her on, despite the lack of compassion and regular criticism of her physique. Pictured: Karen, Daniel Lee and Donatella Versace at The Fashion Awards 2019

Her eye-opening book, which she says is not the 'typical model book', reveals the behind-the-scenes of the fashion industry and details some of the more unpleasant experiences she went through while making a name for herself. Pictured: Karen at the Victor Alfaro fashion show at New York Fashion Week 2000/2001

She said: 'When I'm thin, I'm successful. But when I'm 10lb or 15lb heavier than what they want, that means I'm not worthy. 

'To be successful, I had to play the game, it's the unspoken rules of the business.'

At 19, she was also mocked by a designer at Milan fashion week and axed from the show. 

Soon other other designers caught wind of her being dropped and they cut her out of every show she was due to appear in too. 

She recalls a time at one of her first fashion shoots where several people called her 'fatty' after an editor told her she had eaten too much when a dress didn't fit her. Pictured: Karen with Beth Ditto and Boy George at Paris Fashion Week 2011

During another unpleasant experience, she says she was mocked by a designer at Milan Fashion Week and axed from their show before every other designed got wind and followed suit. Pictured: Karen walking in Milan Fashion week in February

The redhead says the process of being let go lead to a 'cycle of disordered eating' as designers would pick her up for shows again when she lost weight. Pictured: Karen and Alexa Chung at the Costume Institute Gala Benefit (the former name of the Met Ball) in 2012 

As a result she became extremely depressed but was picked up by designers again after she lost weight - which she says sparked a 'cycle of disordered eating'. 

She reveals her experiences with body shaming still influence her to this day, with lockdown being the first opportunity she has taken to eat what she wanted without fearing for her career.

Instead, she and her children, Scarlett, 14, and Henry, 13, who she had with ex-husband Jack White of band The White Stripes, have spent their time watching the Great British Bake Off. 

She hopes her working-class background, as well as her ex-husband's, will help keep her children grounded.  

She shares her two children, Scarlett, 14, and Henry, 13, with her ex-husband Jack White of band White Stripes. Pictured: Karen and Jack White at the Met Ball in 2008

She has spent her time during lockdown watching the Great British Bake Off with her children at their home in Nashville. Pictured: Karen modelling for Chanel in Paris Fashion Show 1997

Karen has been on more than 30 international covers of Vogue, featured in the 2012 Olympics closing ceremony and has been pictured by some of the famous fashion photographers such as Steven Meisel and Peter Lindbergh. 

While her book discusses the unspoken side of the fashion industry, it also touches on her childhood growing up in a working class family from Oldham, Lancashire. 

Karen, who now lives in Nashville with her children and their four cats Annie, Fergus, Marmalade and Jam, speaks candidly about her upbringing, with her twin sister Kate and two older half-brothers, after her parents divorced when she was seven. 

On occasions, her mother would collect pennies out of the urinals in the pub she worked at, disinfect them and then give them to her girls for pocket money. 

Karen has been on more than 30 international covers of Vogue and even featured in the 2012 Olympics closing ceremony. Pictured: Karen walking in New York Fashion Week 2011

The book also touches on her childhood growing up in a working class family from Oldham, Lancashire. Pictured: Karen on the catwalk for the Rag and Bone show in New York Fashion Week 2020

Before the age of 15, she lived a normal life, but set her sights on modelling after reading Just Seventeen magazine and signing up to Boss model agency in Manchester. 

By the age of 16 she was on a train to London after leaving school and was soon propelled into the fashion spotlight.

But it was not without its setbacks and difficulties, attending casting from nine in the morning and finishing up at seven at night. 

Karen battled for several years to make a name for herself and found success in New York on her 18th birthday, with an iconic shoot with makeup styled by Pat McGrath. 

For the eye-catching look she had her eyebrows shaved off and her hair was roughly chopped and dyed a deep red.   

From then on she knew she wanted to be part of something different and now she only walks in shows for designers who are willing to fit clothes to her size.
