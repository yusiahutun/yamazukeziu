How the 'Kate effect' turns brands into success stories overnight

When it comes to viral fashion moments and sell-out products, it isn't social media stars or even celebrities who are the most powerful (and enduring) influencers. In fact, the style icons to whom we have all looked for centuries aren't even involved in the fashion industry at all.

The royals have been popularising certain pieces since time in memoriam, from Princess Alexandra's tailored looks to Princess Diana's pie-crust collars. Then as now, people clamoured to copy the styles they saw on their favourite duchesses and princesses, leading to the formation of trends and the hunt for specific, sought-after pieces. These days, the 'Kate effect' is the nickname given to the economic influence the Princess of Wales wields – which, in recent years, has been estimated to give a £1 billion annual boost to the UK fashion industry. At the coronation this weekend, it's fair to say all eyes were on what Kate wore.

Although a brand worn by any member of the royal family can expect an uptick in interest and sales, the royals who really assure a flurry of press and a months-long waiting list are the Princess of Wales and the Duchess of Sussex. "The increase in brand awareness and enquiries was immediate, not just in the UK but especially in the US," says designer Jennifer Chamandi, whose elegant heels have been worn by both royals. Meghan chose a pair of two-tone pumps with the brand's signature strap for a visit to the Robert Clack School in Dagenham in 2020 and Kate soon followed suit, sporting the Lorenzo 105 black suede pumps for a visit to the V&A museum in 2021.

According to Chamandi, the 'Kate effect' was instantaneous. "We had messages from stockists globally, wanting to know how quickly we could deliver a shipment of the style Kate wore," she recalls. "Being a British brand, having a member of the royal family wear my shoes was a dream come true. After another intense lockdown, it felt like a ray of optimism."

Predominantly, interest in the brand came from the US, which overtook the UK as the biggest market for orders directly from Chamandi's website. A Newsweek poll earlier this year revealed William and Kate to be the most popular royals stateside, meaning that Kate's outfits have an incredible influence on shoppers across the pond. It's a phenomenon that the shoe designer Camilla Elphick experienced when Kate first wore a pair of her Alicia pumps in 2022, to a charity polo match.

Kate wearing the Alicia Camilla Elphick pumps for a charity polo match in 2022

"It was a normal day in the office, and suddenly I got a flurry of calls from my mum and friends," she explains. "I had no idea [Kate] was going to wear them. The phones didn't stop ringing all day. We've got a live chat on our website, and it just went off the scale – especially in America. At the time, the style was our most popular anyway, but when people saw Kate wearing it, it completely sold out."

Max Mumby/Indigo

The then-duchess went on to wear the shoes again for the Commonwealth Games a few months later, which solidified them as a must-have piece for royal style watchers. "It's now become an iconic style for us," says Elphick. "I think the fact that Kate wore them on two different occasions – once with a dress and once with trousers – showed people how useful they are. We make them in a whole range now, and we have a customer in Dubai who has ever single colour we've ever made."

Kate's shoes are perhaps the most sure-fire of sell-outs. The first pair she sent viral were from a then-relatively unknown London brand, Penelope Chilvers, whose Long Tassel boots she first wore to a fair in 2004; nearly 1o years later, she still brings them out regularly. In fact, the boots have become so synonymous with Kate that they've been worn on set by Meg Bellamy, the actress playing Kate in the upcoming series of The Crown.

PA Images

Meg Bellamy pictured on the set of The Crown, wearing the Penelope Chilvers boots Kate is known to love

"Kate has been a loyal customer for many years now," says Chilvers. "In fact, she was one of my very first customers, together with her mother and sister. There was a wonderful image of her, perching on a shooting stick, that was used by all the papers to spread the news of her engagement to Prince William."

"It was an introduction to a beautiful young girl who was to be the future Queen of England, and it was circumstantial that she was wearing our boots," she continues. "But looking back, it was a characterful shot of her, giving away so many of her qualities as a role model: teaching the world to slow down their consumerist habits by wearing her wardrobe more than once, making it very clear that she thinks that 'not new' is OK, and showing her continuous support of British designers."

It was one of the first times a young Kate Middleton had caused a rush on a particular piece. "I was still starting out at the time, and the phone didn't stop ringing for days," says Chilvers. "And each time Kate wears her 'old faithfuls', yes, the sales go up." Does she really still wear the same pair she bought all those years ago? "Of course! Our boots last a lifetime with a bit of spit and polish."

"Being worn by a royal definitely has the power to raise brand profile as well as awareness"

Kate isn't the only one in whom shoppers are interested, of course. The Duchess of Sussex – who arguably understands a good photo opportunity better than any of the other royals – is a fan of jewellery designer Roxanne First. She wore the brand's Snake hoop earrings to the Endeavour Awards with Prince Harry in 2020, which was the couple's final appearance as working senior royals. Images of them, holding hands under an umbrella as rain lashed down, became some of the most famous ever taken of the pair, and were beamed around the world.

Max Mumby/Indigo//Getty Images

The Duchess of Sussex at the Endeavour Awards, wearing Roxanne First

"It was quite an iconic set of images, which did go fairly viral at the time," says First. "I love seeing our jewellery being worn by all our clients, but it felt especially good to see the duchess wearing these hoops at such an important event. The next day, we were inundated with orders for these gold hoops, and a clear majority of sales came from the US. It was a phenomenal amount of orders!"

Sales for the hoops increased by 500 per cent after Meghan wore them, and the US is now a top-three market for the brand. "Being worn by a royal definitely has the power to raise brand profile as well as awareness," says First. "It's also a huge compliment to be worn by one of the royals, especially as we are fairly young brand." First only launched her brand in 2018; now, she's stocked by all the major retailers worldwide.

Jewellery designer Pippa Small also noticed an incredible increase in sales after Meghan made a surprise appearance at the Fashion Awards in 2018, when she was pregnant with Archie. "When the Duchess of Sussex wore our Afghan bangles at the awards, we only found out when our emails started going crazy," she says. "There was an immediate pick up in media interest. It's been amazing, because it's given us visibility and our artisans prestige."

Pool/Samir Hussein

Meghan in her favourite Pippa Small necklace, which she wore for both a royal tour and her interview with Oprah

Meghan has worn Small's pieces on a number of occasions; she paired her three-stone Aquamarine Amulet necklace with a blue dress on a tour of the Pacific in 2018, and wore it again for her infamous interview with Oprah Winfrey in 2021. "That caused such a spike in sales," recalls Small. "We rushed to order the stones used in the necklace to fulfil all the orders, as soon as we saw in the trailer that she had chosen to wear it again."

"There was an immediate pick up in media interest"

Sometimes, one royal can catapult a brand into becoming a stalwart for the rest of the family. One such label is the Edinburgh-based accessories brand, Strathberry, which was first worn by Meghan, but has gone on to be favoured by Kate, Princess Eugenie and Zara Tindall.

"People are just so intrigued by the royals and the way they present themselves," says co-founder Leeanne Hundleby, who started Strathberry in 2013 with her husband Guy. "Being worn by the royals has opened up whole new audiences for us, and introduced us to people who had never heard of the brand before."

Meghan, who has at least three of the brand's bags, wore the Midi tote for her first ever royal walkabout with Prince Harry in Nottingham in 2017, shortly after the couple got engaged. Kate has followed suit, carrying the Multrees wallet on several occasions, while Zara Tindall has worn the East/West baguette clutch more than once – in cream for the royal family's Easter Sunday church service earlier this year and in blue at the King's coronation ceremony. Princess Eugenie, meanwhile, was recently seen in Bahrain watching the Grand Prix, wearing a Topshop duster coat and Strathberry's Lana Osette Midi bucket bag.

Karwai Tang//Getty Images

Zara Tindall carrying a Strathberry clutch at the coronation

"It all started when Meghan first carried one of our bags," explains Guy. "It was such a big thing at the time. That particular bag kept selling out; thankfully, we had the capacity to expand. It was a bit of a challenge, and it was a good few months until we caught up. We'd been growing prior to that but, after that day, we doubled in size over the next year."

Jane Barlow

Kate carrying Strathberry’s Multrees wallet

Why do they think the royals are so hooked on their brand? "We are very focused on craftsmanship and quality, but we still sell at an affordable price," says Leeanne. "We make our products alongside Loewe, Dior and Caroline Herrera, who use the same workshops and leather as us. But we're up in Edinburgh, a bit removed from the fashion centres, and we try to be original in what we create."

The stardust the royals can bestow on a brand isn't, however, a guaranteed golden ticket. Issa London was a relatively niche label before a young Kate Middleton wore several of its pieces, eventually choosing a navy blue wrap dress for her engagement announcement to Prince William in 2010 – a moment that turned everything upside down. "In 2008 and 2009 we were in serious financial trouble," Helayel has previously said. "When Kate wore that dress everything changed."

"From the day of the royal engagement our sales doubled – but I didn’t have the money to finance production on that scale. The bank refused to give me credit, and the factory was screaming for me to pay its bills. I needed an investor."

Over the next five years, the brand struggled with a new leadership team and Helayel's departure. Camilla Al-Fayed became the company's new majority stakeholder and, eventually, Issa shuttered in December 2015.

Chris Jackson//Getty Images

Kate wearing an Issa London dress for her engagement announcement in 2010

For Strathberry's Guy Hundleby, the key to staying on top of the 'Kate effect' – and, really, the effect every royal bestows – lies in building longevity beyond certain pieces and fads. "You’ve got to be careful, because if you build too much on something like that, it can create a risk," he says. "Inevitably, the hype calms down a bit, and you need to make sure the brand is building up alongside that incredible opportunity. It’s a fantastic moment in time, but it doesn’t define you – it’s just part of your journey."
